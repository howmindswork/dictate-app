Dictation Software With Fully Custom Hotkeys — No Restrictions

TechTips  ·  May 2026  ·  5 min read

Most dictation tools give you one activation method. Maybe it is a specific key. Maybe it is a click on a floating button. Either way, it is fixed. You use it their way or you do not use it at all.

That is a small thing until it is not. If the default hotkey conflicts with something you use constantly, every dictation session becomes a friction point.

Why Hotkeys Matter

A hotkey that conflicts with your workflow does not get used. Simple as that. The best dictation tool in the world is useless if activating it requires you to stop what you are doing and hunt for the right gesture.

The tools that lock hotkeys know this. It is sometimes a monetization strategy — pay for the premium tier to get customization. Other times it is just laziness in the product design.

dictate.app: Any Key, Any Combination

dictate.app defaults to Ctrl+Shift+Space. That is a comfortable hold that does not conflict with most applications.

Do not like it? Change it. Open settings, click the hotkey field, press your preferred combination, save. Done. Zero restrictions. No premium tier required.

Want to use a single key? Bind it. A mouse button? Works. A media key? Works. Whatever fits your hands and workflow, you can set it.

Hotkey Conflicts: What to Watch For

Some popular combinations are already claimed by other software:

  • Ctrl+Space — used by many code editors for autocomplete
  • Windows+H — Windows 11 built-in speech recognition
  • Ctrl+Shift+I — browser developer tools

With dictate.app, you check your options once and pick something clean. No workarounds. No worrying about which application "wins" the keybind.

Push-to-Talk vs Toggle

dictate.app uses push-to-talk. Hold the hotkey to record. Release to transcribe. This is intentional. It keeps recordings clean and deliberate. No accidental dictation when you leave the app open. No background noise captured while you are thinking.

Who Benefits from Custom Hotkeys

  • Left-handed users who find default right-hand combinations awkward
  • Ergonomic keyboard users with non-standard layouts
  • Gamers who have specific keys reserved for other software
  • Accessibility users who need specific physical accommodations
  • Power users who manage ten tools and cannot afford conflicts
